The problem

Two problems, really — one about heat, one about drowning in data.

The dataset behind this project — Urban Heat / Urban Sales (UHUS) — pairs Seoul's daily weather and card-sales records with urban-context features and a machine-learning explanation of them. Buried inside was a question worth surfacing.

The analytical question

Does extreme heat actually suppress retail activity in Seoul — and if so, where, for which kinds of business, and alongside which characteristics of a neighborhood? A single citywide average hides all of that.

The build challenge

Turn weather, sales, features, SHAP explanations, and geometry into something a non-expert can explore spatially and through time — without burying them under every number at once.

Both problems shaped every decision that follows. The first drove the analytics and the map; the second drove an information hierarchy that took several iterations to get right. This page walks through both.

The anchor metric

Everything hangs off one number: RHSI.

Rather than let users wade through raw sales, the atlas is organized around a single derived index — the Retail Heat-Sensitivity Index. It compares what a neighborhood sells on scorching days against a mild baseline, on a log scale so rises and falls read symmetrically.

RHSI = log ( mean retail sales on hot days ÷ mean retail sales on mild days ) A hot day = apparent temp ≥ 33°C · A mild day = 18–26°C, no rain, non-holiday
RHSI < 0 — sales fall on heat (heat-sensitive) 0 RHSI > 0 — sales hold or rise

When the interface needs a plain-language number, it converts the index to an approximate percentage change with % ≈ (exp(RHSI) − 1) × 100. The same metric feeds the map color, the KPI readouts, the histogram, and the district rankings — so wherever you are, you're reading the same yardstick.

Stated plainly in the product

RHSI is an association, not a cause. It describes an observed hot-vs-mild difference; it does not prove heat is the reason. That honesty is built into the copy, not hidden in a footnote.

Data foundation

A Python pipeline bakes everything down to static files.

The single biggest architectural bet: there is no backend. Every aggregate, statistic, and SHAP summary is precomputed offline by a handful of Python scripts and written to static JSON. The browser just fetches and draws. No server, no database, no API to keep alive — the whole thing hosts on plain static storage.

What the pipeline does

prepare_data.py
Simplifies 422 dong polygons, dissolves them into 25 gu, joins RHSI + features + SHAP by dong_code, and computes hot/mild industry statistics and daily series.
prepare_sales_groups.py
Maps ~84 industry columns into six retail themes and writes the per-gu and per-dong daily group series.
fetch_osm_roads.py
Pulls arterial + mid-tier roads from OpenStreetMap via Overpass and simplifies them.
fetch_osm_buildings.py
Fetches building footprints one district at a time (a citywide query is too heavy), with retries and resumable progress.
tag_buildings.py
Uses a Shapely spatial index to stamp each building with its gu/dong code — the key that later lets the map draw only one district's buildings.

Architecture

Six plain-JavaScript modules, no build step.

The frontend is deliberately low-ceremony: vanilla JavaScript in global modules loaded in dependency order, so editing means refresh the browser — no compile, no bundler. Each module owns one concern, and data.js sits at the base feeding everything above it.

Why deck.gl is overlaid, not interleaved

The map is MapLibre GL for the basemap with a deck.gl overlay for every data layer. deck.gl is layered on top of MapLibre rather than woven into its render pipeline — a deliberate call. Interleaving large heatmaps and additive glow with the tile pipeline produces depth-buffer and ordering artifacts; overlaying avoids them while still letting extruded buildings self-occlude through deck's own depth buffer.

Hard problems & how they were solved

Five decisions that made or broke the atlas.

Every interesting build has a handful of moments where the obvious approach fails and you have to choose. These were ours.

01 · PERFORMANCE

Drawing a whole city without melting the browser

Problem: the building footprints alone are ~61 MB and hundreds of thousands of polygons. Rendering them citywide tanks the frame rate and can exhaust memory.

Solution: buildings are off by default and hidden below zoom 11.25. The tag_buildings.py step stamps every polygon with a gu/dong code, so when you drill into a district the map draws only that district's buildings. Add simplified + rounded geometry, a static/dynamic layer split, cached transforms, and mid-tier roads that only appear after you drill — and the city stays fluid.

02 · CARTOGRAPHY

Making a signed index legible on a map

Problem: RHSI is signed and skewed — most values cluster just below zero. A naive quantile scale collapses everything into one midpoint color; a linear scale drowns the interesting negatives.

Solution: a sign-aware quantile scale. Negative and non-negative values are ranked separately, with amber pinned at zero — so the meaning of zero is preserved and both the heat-sensitive (rose) and resilient (blue) sides get their full range of contrast.

03 · INTERACTION

Decoupling where you look from what you aggregate

Problem: "zoom level" and "data grain" are different ideas. A user might want the camera on one district but the data aggregated citywide — or vice versa.

Solution: target area and granularity are independent controls. And because aggregating a single district at "Seoul" grain is meaningless, granularity is constrained so it can never be coarser than the target — the invalid options disable themselves and snap to a sensible level.

04 · ANALYTICS

Grounding the charts in the real study, not generic templates

Problem: a first pass reused off-the-shelf chart renderers. They looked fine but said nothing specific — they didn't match what the RHSI research actually found.

Solution: the Insights column was rebuilt directly on the real RHSI, SHAP, and industry data in an editorial-scientific style — each figure is a bold finding sentence, a restrained chart, and a one-line caption. The numbers now match the source study (apparel is the most heat-sensitive retail; ΔDaypop and land price track RHSI most strongly).

05 · TIME

Treating time as a first-class dimension

Problem: a static map can't show that heat and sales are a moving story across 366 days — but re-rendering a full chart every animation frame is far too expensive.

Solution: an animated timeline plays or scrubs the whole year with a temperature line and normalized sales area. The moving day cursor is a cheap incremental update, kept separate from the full chart render — so playback stays smooth while the map's heat field tracks each day's temperature.

The design journey

The second problem: users were being bombarded.

The analytics worked — but early builds showed the same facts three times over. The hover tooltip, the detail panel, and the Insights charts all echoed RHSI, the most-sensitive industry, and the hot/mild day counts. It felt like an information dump. Fixing that took a deliberate information hierarchy.

  • Key indicators moved to the top of Insights — the at-a-glance numbers live in one place, first, where they're noticed.
  • The detail panel went back to exploration only — dataset lineage and definitions, not a repeat of the KPIs.
  • Charts became dataset-specific — the whole project shows its full five-figure story, but selecting a single dataset shows just the 2–3 graphs that fit it, so no view over-explains.
  • Panels are contextual — with nothing selected, Insights and detail step aside and the map takes the whole stage. They appear when you pick something to look at.
The principle underneath

Surface the summary before the detail; let each surface do one job. The map is the star, the KPIs are the glance, the charts are the argument, and the lineage panel is the reference — never all four saying the same thing at once.

What it delivers

Four questions, one place to answer them.

Everything above exists to let someone move fluidly between these four questions — spatially, by sector, by urban characteristic, and through time.

Where
Question 01
Where is retail most sensitive to heat?

Map RHSI at Seoul, district, or neighborhood grain; rank and inspect any dong against the whole city.

Who
Question 02
Which kinds of retail change most on hot days?

Compare mean hot-vs-mild sales across ~84 industries and six themes; find the strongest winners and losers.

Why
Question 03
Which urban characteristics go with the pattern?

Explore land use, accessibility, mobility, and demographics through SHAP contributions and feature-vs-RHSI scatter.

When
Question 04
How do temperature and sales move through 2024?

Play or scrub all 366 days; watch the heat field and six themed sales rings respond day by day.

Kept honest

What it deliberately does not claim.

A research prototype earns trust by being clear about its edges. These limits are stated in the product and its documentation, not buried.

  • Association, not causation — RHSI describes a hot-vs-mild difference; it doesn't identify heat as the cause.
  • Card sales ≠ all activity — and district/city figures use simple means, not transaction- or population-weighting.
  • Per-dong industry detail is the top 20 by volume — the long tail is summarized, not itemized everywhere.
  • The dot-density layer is synthetic — deterministic sample points encoding area-level magnitude, never real stores or people.
  • Building heights are often estimated — from OSM levels or a type-based fallback; visual context, not a survey model.
  • Desktop-first — the three-column analytical layout targets a wide workspace; full mobile and accessibility work remain open.
